## Block 2 - The city as an idea (origin)

**Eyebrow/label:** The origin

**Headline:** A city that survived by connecting.

**Body:**
Mexico City should not work. Twenty-one million people, layer on layer: Aztec foundations under colonial stone under glass towers. For a while the world knew it for one word, murder capital, and wrote it off.

It did not stay written off. It became one of the most alive places on earth, not by tearing down the old to make room for the new, but by binding them. Ancient markets next to design studios. Street vendors next to startups. Nothing was thrown away; everything was wired together. The city did not grow by addition. It grew by connection.

That is the whole idea behind this practice. Most companies are not short on talent or tools. The parts are usually fine on their own. They just do not talk. Connect the silos and the same parts start to multiply. CDMX is named after the city for exactly this reason. The work is the wiring.

**Microcopy/CTA:**
(none)

**Notes for design:**
This is the heart. Give it room and a slow scroll. Consider a subtle parallax of the three city layers, stone, market, glass, stacking as the reader descends. Keep type large and calm. No stat clutter here; the numbers come next.

## Block 4 - The model

**Eyebrow/label:** The model

**Headline:** One head that connects three domains.

**Body:**
Most growth problems do not live in one department. They live in the gaps between them: marketing that does not talk to strategy, strategy that does not talk to the systems that run the business. The usual answer is to hire three specialists who each own a corner. Three roadmaps that rarely meet, and the space between them is where growth leaks out.

This is not an agency, which sells you a team and splits your problem across it. It is not a fractional CMO, who advises and then hands the doing to others. It is not three hires, who each own a slice. It is one operator who holds three domains at once: Digital Growth, Business Strategy, AI and Automation. Held by one head, they stop being separate budgets and start being one motion. The strategy knows what the growth engine can actually do. The growth engine knows what the AI can automate. The automation knows what the strategy is for. That is the difference between three good answers and one that compounds.

The model is deliberately small. One operator, one client at a time, a one-year embedded engagement, working alongside the teams, agencies and vendors already in place. Not above them. Among them. The model itself is the argument.

**The three domains:**
- Digital Growth. The demand, the channels, the revenue you can attribute.
- Business Strategy. Where the company is going and what it says no to.
- AI and Automation. Production systems that take work off people, shipped, not promised.

**Microcopy/CTA:**
Three domains. One line through them.

**Notes for design:**
Show the three domains as three points that the reader can see connect into a single path, not a three-column grid of separate services. Lines between the three labels, meeting at a center point. The center point is the operator. The contrast (not an agency, not a fractional CMO, not three hires) is load-bearing; keep it visible and scannable. Reinforce that the value is the connection, not the list.

## Block 5 - The connection (interaction)

**Eyebrow/label:** Try it

**Headline:** Silos do nothing alone. Connect them.

**Body:**
Three points of light sit apart on the screen, each one a domain that usually runs on its own budget and its own calendar. Draw a line between two and something happens that neither could do alone. Connect all three and the whole thing comes alive, the way a dark city does when its grid finally wakes. Nothing here is new. It was all already in the building. It just was not connected yet.

**Microcopy/CTA:**
Drag to connect the silos. (On touch: tap each point to link them.)

**Notes for design:**
Interactive block. Three nodes labelled Digital Growth, Business Strategy, AI and Automation. As the visitor links them, the city behind brightens and a short result line resolves, and completing all three resolves into the CDMX x-glyph, closing the metaphor and tying to the live brand. Keep the framing copy to two sentences; the interaction is the message. Provide a non-interactive fallback for reduced-motion and keyboard users that still shows the three domains connecting.

## Block 6 - Growing people

**Eyebrow/label:** The point of it

**Headline:** When the work connects, people grow with it.

**Body:**
A city is not its skyline. It is the people who get to stand in it with their heads up. Mexico City came back because connection gave ordinary people a place to belong and a reason to be proud of where they were from.

The same is true inside a company. When disciplines stop working in isolation, the people inside them stop being cogs in a corner and start seeing the whole picture they are part of. The growth marketer finally understands the strategy they serve. The team that was carrying a problem alone realizes it was never theirs to carry alone. A year embedded means the skills stay long after the mandate closes. The company compounds because the people compound. They stand in their work, and in their lives, with more pride than when the work began. Both grow, or it did not work.

**Microcopy/CTA:**
(none)

**Notes for design:**
Placement recommendation: keep Block 6 as a standalone block, not woven into Block 4, and place it here, after the model and the interaction, before the KIXX chapter. Standalone gives the human payoff its own beat and a rhythm break after the system-heavy Block 4. It is also the newest, most differentiating idea and the emotional spine the brief asks to lead, so folding it into Block 4 would bury it under the mechanics of the model and dilute both. Visually echo Block 2's city layers, but warm them. Faces or human scale over architecture if imagery is used. Slower pace, warmer typography weight, no metrics.

## Block 9 - Honest filter + CTA

**Eyebrow/label:** Whether this fits

**Headline:** I take one client at a time, so the fit has to be right.

**Body:**
This works for a B2B company of roughly 20 to 200 people, in Belgium or Europe, with a growth problem that crosses more than one domain, and a founder or managing director who wants one operator accountable for the whole line rather than three vendors pointing at each other.

It does not work as a quick campaign, a single channel fixed, or a body for hire under someone else's plan. It does not fit anyone who wants ten clients served at once; the model is one client at a time, and that constraint is the whole value. Those are valid needs. They are just a different model than mine.

One operator can hold one client at a time, so the next opening is a real constraint, not a sales line. New mandates by waitlist. The current slot is taken. The next one is decided in advance, by the people who start talking early. If the fit is right, the next step is a conversation, not a pitch. Tell me where the lines are broken and we will see whether this is the work.

**Microcopy/CTA:**
Start the conversation: renke@cdmx.be

**Notes for design:**
Two clear halves: this is for, this is not for. The honesty is the persuasion; do not soften the "not for you" side. CTA is a single calm email line, no booking widget. The waitlist reality (one client at a time) does the urgency work for the prospect without a countdown or a scarcity badge.
